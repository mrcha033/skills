# Numbers-free stock spectacle contract

## Reader-facing structure

### Title

Use a vivid, number-free title that makes the company feel like the protagonist of a tale worth hearing. Do not assert an outcome.

### Roll up, roll up

Open in a flamboyant market-showman voice. Introduce what the company claims to change in the world and why believers cannot stop talking about it. Do not mention the upstream report or any numerical fact.

### Meet the cast

Bring in founders or management, customers, products, competitors, suppliers, regulators, and the wider market as characters and forces. Use only sourced factual foundations. Never invent dialogue, private motives, or unseen events.

### The marvelous machine

Explain the business and product magic in concrete qualitative terms: what goes in, what comes out, who depends on it, why it could become difficult to replace, and where the trick may fail.

### The dream they are selling

Tell the most compelling version of the company's future. Make it exhilarating while marking causal interpretation as inference and future events as scenario.

### The trap beneath the carpet

Reveal the strongest qualitative countercase: execution fragility, customer skepticism, competitive attack, policy pressure, supply dependence, management credibility, narrative crowding, or another sourced weakness.

### Three possible endings

- **The coronation** — the qualitative chain that would make the grand promise feel real.
- **The endless rehearsal** — the qualitative chain that keeps the story alive but never fully proven.
- **The vanishing act** — the qualitative chain that makes the spectacle unravel.

Make all three endings distinct and plausible. Do not assign probabilities, rankings, prices, dates, quantities, or numerical triggers.

### The final plot twist

End with the single most surprising sourced fact or clearly labeled inference against the most seductive version of the story.

### What to watch backstage

Name only qualitative signals: management language changing, customers behaving differently, a product becoming indispensable, competitors copying the play, regulators tightening the stage, suppliers gaining leverage, or the narrative losing its audience. Do not include thresholds or calendar dates.

## Style

- Sound like a gifted carnival barker who also happens to be an honest investigative reporter.
- Be theatrical, funny, vivid, and rhythmically varied.
- Use metaphors as entertainment, never as evidence.
- Keep the hype in the voice, not in fabricated facts.
- Never use digits, quantities, prices, dates, scores, percentages, ratios, rankings, numbered lists, or written-out numerical comparisons.
- Cite factual paragraphs with descriptive Markdown links whose visible labels contain no numbers.
- Do not append a visible evidence ledger; keep verification backstage.
- Close with a brief number-free sentence stating that the performance is entertainment and scenario storytelling, not personalized investment advice or an execution instruction.
